# Content — How to Communicate with AI (Basic Level)

---

# Objective

By the end of this course, you'll be able to talk to AI like someone who knows how to give clear instructions: with clarity, context, and control over the result. That means fewer attempts, less frustration, and more useful answers.

AI isn't magic: it responds better when you get better at asking. Learning to prompt is learning to think with order.

---

# Remember, This Is Not Google

Google searches pages; AI builds a response from what you give it.

- With Google: you type keywords and you do the work of interpreting results.
- With AI: you give instructions and AI does the work of organizing, drafting, summarizing, proposing, comparing, etc.

**The most important difference:** Google finds what already exists. AI builds something new from what you tell it. The clearer you are, the better the result.

# Skills Map — What You'll Master After This Course

## 1. Clarity = Ask Well
- **What it is:** saying exactly what you need, without ambiguity.
- **If missing:** generic answers.
- **How to fix it:** define the final deliverable (what output do you want).
- **Example:** "Help me with this" → "Give me 7 actionable steps + a final checklist"

## 2. Context = Give What's Needed
- **What it is:** minimum data so AI doesn't have to guess.
- **If missing:** AI invents or assumes.
- **How to fix it:** add "situation + objective + audience."
- **Example:** "Write a post" → "Instagram post for [audience], about [topic], goal [action], with [tone]"

## 3. Format = Control the Output
- **What it is:** asking for the answer with a specific structure.
- **If missing:** long, messy text.
- **How to fix it:** "Deliver it as: …"
- **Example:** "Give me ideas" → "Give me 10 ideas in a table with: Title / Benefit / First Step"

## 4. Constraints = Tone, Length, Rules
- **What it is:** limits to avoid filler or tangents.
- **If missing:** it goes off track.
- **How to fix it:** tone + length + "don't do X."
- **Example:** "Explain this to me" → "In 120 words, no jargon, with 1 analogy"

## 5. Iteration = Improve in the 2nd/3rd Message
- **What it is:** adjusting like you're directing someone's work.
- **If missing:** frustration ("it doesn't work").
- **How to fix it:** specific feedback: "make it more…, change to…, avoid…"
- **Wildcard:** "Before answering, if anything is missing, ask me 3 questions."

## 6. Verification = Avoid Hallucinations
- **What it is:** asking AI to flag assumptions and verify when relevant.
- **If missing:** blind trust in questionable answers.
- **How to fix it:** "if you don't know, say so; suggest how to verify it."
- **Template:** "If you're not sure, tell me and give me a way to check."

## 7. Templates = Repeat Results
- **What it is:** reusable prompts for recurring tasks.
- **If missing:** starting from zero every time.
- **How to fix it:** save a fixed structure and only change the variables.

> **Visual suggestion — Skills Wheel or Pyramid infographic**
> A circular wheel with 7 segments, one per skill, color-coded. Center label: "Great Prompt." Each segment shows: skill name + one-word descriptor (Clarity = Ask Well, etc.). Alternatively, a 3-tier pyramid: base = Clarity + Context, middle = Format + Constraints + Iteration, top = Verification + Templates. Add a small "if missing → result" tooltip for each skill.

# Multimodality

This module can be **very eye-opening** for beginners because it teaches something key: _there is no single "type" of prompting_. There are **channels** (text, voice, images) and you choose the one that lets you deliver better **context + control** with less friction.

AI doesn't understand you better because it's text or voice: it understands you better when your message has clarity, context, and format. Multimodality just helps you _give that information faster or more completely_.

## Text — When You Need Precision and Control
- Most controllable channel
- Allows copy/paste, versioning, and saving templates
- When you want exact format (table, checklist, script, email)
- When you need clear constraints (tone, length, "don't do X")
- When you want to reuse the prompt as a template

## Voice — When You Need Speed and Context Building
- Good for "thinking out loud" and giving AI a lot of context quickly
- When you're exploring ideas
- When you have a lot of context in your head and writing it feels like too much effort
- When you need to "build" the request through conversation

**Recommended process for building your prompt with voice:**
1. Tell the AI: "I'm going to explain what I need by voice. Don't answer yet. When I say 'DONE', do this: (a) summarize my context in bullets, (b) ask me questions to fill in what's missing."
2. Speak freely. Say "DONE" when you're finished.
3. Verify that AI has the correct and complete context.
4. Ask it to help you build the ideal prompt with a clear action toward your final goal. Remember: you can pause the voice conversation and continue by text when you need precision.

> **2025–2026 Note:** Voice AI models have improved dramatically. ChatGPT Advanced Voice, Gemini Live, and Claude Voice already understand tone nuances, interruptions, and extended context. Use them to explore; switch to text to define.

## Images — When the Image IS the Context

Using images with AI isn't just "describing a photo." It's giving information that would take too long to write.

**When to use images as part of your prompt:**
- You have a document, screen, or screenshot you want AI to analyze
- You want AI to reproduce or improve a design, table, or visual structure
- You're describing a screen error or a visual problem
- You have a chart and want AI to interpret it or build arguments from it

**Practical examples:**

| Without image | With image |
|---|---|
| "I have a sales table by month, how do I improve it?" | [attach the table] + "Suggest 3 clearer ways to visualize this data" |
| "The system gives me an error, what do I do?" | [attach screenshot] + "What does this error mean and what's the first step to fix it?" |
| "How do I create this email design?" | [attach the design] + "Write the text for each section following this layout" |

**Golden rule for images:** The image provides the "what" visually; you still need to give the "why" in text.

> **2025–2026 Note:** GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5, and Claude 3.7+ process images, PDFs, screenshots, and even short videos. You no longer need to transcribe a document — you can paste it directly and ask about it.

> **Visual suggestion — Multimodality channel selector**
> Three-panel horizontal card (or three cards): Text / Voice / Images. Each panel has: icon, one-line description, 3 best use cases, and a visual indicator of "control level" (e.g., a slider or 1–3 bars). Text = high control, Voice = high speed, Images = high context. Works great as a printable reference or course slide.

# Formulas (Acronyms)

Formulas are structures that help you remember the key ingredients of a good prompt. You don't need to use all of them — pick the one that fits the type of task.

## Universal Formula — PROMPT

Use it for any type of request when you don't know where to start.

| Letter | Component | Question it answers |
|---|---|---|
| **P** | Purpose | What do I need this for? What's the goal? |
| **R** | Role | What role do I want AI to play? |
| **O** | Output | What form should the answer take? |
| **M** | More context | What extra information does AI need? |
| **P** | Parameters | What constraints apply? (tone, length, format) |
| **T** | Test | How will I know if the answer is good? |

**Applied example:**
> "Act as a career coach (R). I need to prepare for a job interview for a sales manager position (P). Give me a list of the 10 most common interview questions + a recommended answer for each (O). The company is a growing B2B tech startup (M). Answers in max 3 sentences, professional but direct tone (P). A good answer demonstrates leadership and concrete metrics (T)."

> **Visual suggestion — PROMPT formula card**
> Vertical card (business card format or A5) with each letter as a row: colored letter on the left, component name in bold, question it answers in light text. Color gradient from top to bottom. Works as a printed cheat sheet or course slide. Add a small "filled example" on the back/bottom showing the formula in action.

## By Task Type

### TEACH — Explain / Learn Something

| Letter | Component |
|---|---|
| **T** | Topic — What do I want to understand? |
| **E** | Experience — How much do I already know about this? |
| **A** | Analogy — Ask for an analogy or real-world example |
| **C** | Check — How do I know I understood? (ask me at the end) |
| **H** | How deep — Intro, intermediate, or expert level? |

**Example:**
> "Explain what generative artificial intelligence is (T). I'm an elementary school teacher with no technical background (E). Use a cooking analogy (A). At the end, ask me 3 questions to verify I understood (C). Introductory level, no formulas (H)."

---

### CRAFT — Write / Draft

| Letter | Component |
|---|---|
| **C** | Context — What is the text for? Who is it for? |
| **R** | Role — What voice or style should it have? |
| **A** | Action — What do I want the reader to do? |
| **F** | Format — How should it look? (length, structure, tone) |
| **T** | Taboos — What should it NOT include? |

**Example:**
> "Write a post-meeting follow-up email (C). Professional but friendly voice, like a trusted consultant (R). I want the client to confirm the next 30-min call this week (A). Maximum 120 words, with a summary paragraph and a closing (F). Don't use phrases like 'hope this finds you well' or emojis (T)."

---

### MAPS — Plan

| Letter | Component |
|---|---|
| **M** | Mission — What is the final goal? |
| **A** | Actors — Who is involved? |
| **P** | Period — How much time is there? What constraints exist? |
| **S** | Steps — Ask for a step-by-step plan with concrete deliverables |

**Example:**
> "I need to launch a new product on social media (M). The team is 2 people: me (marketing) and a designer (A). We have 3 weeks and zero paid advertising budget (P). Give me a week-by-week plan with tasks, owner, and deliverable for each stage (S)."

---

### DEBUG — Fix a Response

Use when AI gave you something that wasn't what you expected.

| Letter | Component |
|---|---|
| **D** | Describe the problem — What went wrong? |
| **E** | Expected — How should it have looked? |
| **B** | Background — What context was missing or confusing? |
| **U** | Update — What specifically needs to change? |
| **G** | Go — Ask again with the correction incorporated |

**Example:**
> "The response you gave me is too formal and long (D). I needed something casual, like I'd write it myself on Instagram (E). I forgot to tell you it's for my personal account, not corporate (B). Shorten it to 3 sentences and use a more relaxed tone (U). Now rewrite it (G)."

---

### SLIM — Summarize / Extract Information

| Letter | Component |
|---|---|
| **S** | Source — What document or text do I want summarized? |
| **L** | Length — How long should the summary be? |
| **I** | Intent — What will I use the summary for? |
| **M** | Must include — What can't be left out? |

**Example:**
> "Here's yesterday's meeting notes [paste text] (S). Summary in max 5 bullets (L). I'm going to share it with the team that wasn't present (I). It must include: decisions made, owners, and next steps with dates (M)."

> **Visual suggestion — Formula reference card set**
> One card per formula (TEACH, CRAFT, MAPS, DEBUG, SLIM), same layout: formula name + acronym breakdown as a vertical list + one mini example per card. Design as a set of 5 matching cards (same color scheme, different accent color per formula). Print front/back or use as digital sticky notes. A single "formula selector" flowchart also works: "What kind of task is it?" → branches to the right formula.

# The Second Message Guide (Iteration)

The first message is the start, not the finish. Working with AI is like working with an assistant: the first deliverable gives you information to improve the next one.

**Key principle:** Don't abandon a bad prompt. Fix it with specific instructions.

## Most useful iteration phrases

| Goal | Phrase |
|---|---|
| Shorten | "Make it shorter. Maximum [X] words/sentences." |
| Go deeper | "Expand on point [X] with more detail." |
| Change tone | "Rewrite it with a [more formal / more casual / more direct] tone." |
| Change format | "Change the format to [table / bullets / step-by-step / email]." |
| Get options | "Give me 3 different versions of this." |
| Prevent errors | "Before answering, if anything is missing, ask me 3 questions." |
| Verify | "What did you assume to write this? List your main assumptions." |
| Avoid hallucinations | "Don't make up data. If you're not sure about something, tell me and suggest how to verify it." |
| Reset | "Ignore your previous answer. Start over with these instructions: [...]" |

## The 3-Message Flow

A good result usually takes 2–3 messages, not 1.

```
Message 1: Ask with context and format
        ↓
Message 2: Fix what didn't work (tone, length, structure)
        ↓
Message 3: Fine-tune or ask for alternatives
```

> When you reach the third message without a satisfying result, the problem is usually in Message 1's context. Start over with more information.

> **Visual suggestion — Iteration flow diagram**
> Horizontal 3-step flow with arrows: Message 1 → Message 2 → Message 3. Under each step, a small card with what to include (M1: context + format / M2: what to fix + how / M3: fine-tune or alternatives). Add a "restart" arrow looping back from M3 to M1 with the label "if still off: add more context." Use muted background colors with bold step numbers.

# Safe and Realistic Use Rules

AI is a powerful tool. These rules protect you from the most common mistakes.

## 1. Don't share sensitive data
- Full client names, account numbers, passwords, private medical information, or confidential company data **should not be in your prompt**.
- Practical rule: if you wouldn't post it on social media, don't put it in a prompt.
- Alternative: use fictional or generic data. Instead of "my client John Smith with SSN 123-45-6789," write "my client, a 45-year-old entrepreneur in the retail sector."

## 2. Verify important facts
- AIs can "hallucinate": they can invent data, quotes, statistics, or names that sound real but are false.
- Practical rule: if you're going to use data in a presentation, contract, or article, verify it from an official source.
- How to ask AI to help: "If you're not sure about this data, tell me and suggest how I can verify it."

## 3. Ask for sources and flag uncertainty
- You can ask AI to indicate when it's unsure about something.
- Useful prompt: "When you're not certain about a fact, mark the sentence with [verify] at the end."

## 4. AI doesn't replace an expert for critical decisions
- For legal, medical, financial, or security decisions: AI can give you a starting point, but the final decision is made by a human professional.

## 5. Keep your voice
- The biggest risk of using AI for writing is losing your own style. Use it to structure, propose, and edit — not to replace your judgment and perspective.

> **Visual suggestion — Safety rules visual card**
> 5 icons in a row or grid, each representing one rule: lock icon (sensitive data), magnifying glass (verify facts), question mark (flag uncertainty), person with briefcase (consult an expert), microphone (keep your voice). Each icon has a 1-line rule in bold and a 1-line practical tip below. Clean, minimal design. Works as a printed reference card or a slide in the course intro module.

# Cheat Sheet — Quick Reference

## The 7 ingredients of a great prompt

```
1. CLARITY      → Define the exact deliverable
2. CONTEXT      → Situation + objective + audience
3. FORMAT       → How you want to see the answer
4. CONSTRAINTS  → Tone, length, what to avoid
5. ITERATION    → Fix with specific instructions
6. VERIFICATION → Ask AI to flag when it doesn't know
7. TEMPLATE     → Save what works
```

## Emergency formula (when you don't know where to start)

> "Act as [expert in X]. I need [specific task] for [who]. Context: [situation]. Format: [how I want it]. Constraints: [tone / length / what to avoid]."

## Wildcard phrases

- "Before answering, ask me any questions you need."
- "If you're not sure, tell me and suggest how to verify it."
- "Give me 3 versions with different approaches."
- "Make it shorter: maximum [X] words."
- "Rewrite it with a [formal / casual / direct] tone."
- "What did you assume to write this?"

## Debugging tree — If the answer isn't good

```
Is the answer too generic?
    → Missing CONTEXT. Add: who you are, what it's for, who it's for.

Is the answer too long or disorganized?
    → Missing FORMAT. Specify: table / bullets / max X words.

Did the answer go off track?
    → Missing CONSTRAINTS. Add: tone, limits, "don't include X."

Does the answer have data you can't verify?
    → Apply VERIFICATION. Ask it to flag assumptions and sources.

Three messages in and still off?
    → Start over. The problem is in Message 1. Add more context.
```

> **Visual suggestion — Master cheat sheet / poster**
> A single A4 or letter-size poster layout with 4 zones: (1) The 7 ingredients as a numbered list with icons, (2) Emergency formula as a fill-in-the-blank template, (3) Wildcard phrases as a quick-reference box, (4) Debugging tree as a simple flowchart. Use a dark background with light text for "reference poster" feel, or light background with colored section headers for printable handout. This is the key take-home deliverable for the course.
